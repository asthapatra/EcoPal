Deadstock Fabric








Deadstock Fabric: fabric that is left-over or discarded from the past season’s collection in textile mills (often sold in rolls at the fabric store with discounted prices)

Deadstock fabric is often a wild card, in terms of quality, pattern, and stock amounts. However, this type of fabric offers a more environmentally friendly choice for fabrics that would usually end up in landfills. Sourcing deadstock fabric may work better for some brands than others. Deadstock fabric is often found in different stock amounts and patterns due to it being discarded fabric from fabric mills employed by bigger companies. Therefore, smaller brands and companies have more creative control in the fashion pieces that they make out of discarded fabric compared to bigger companies that prefer uniformity in their pieces. Similarly, deadstock fabrics do not require a minimum order quantity which is often a problem for smaller, start-up brands because they are more likely to have a lower stock amount compared to larger brands. While, bigger brands may prefer to have a quick turn-around time on their clothing lines in order to keep up to date with the current fashion trends, smaller brands have the liberty in finding their own niche to differentiate themselves within the small business and fashion community. Furthermore, due to the fact that the fabric is going to be discarded, deadstock fabric is known to be sold at an extremely discounted price.

Despite these benefits, the downsides to using deadstock fabric include lack of traceability, accountability, and variety in fabric choices. Many fabrics that are considered to be deadstock have no trace of where they came from, who produced it, and for what it was discarded for. As a result of the lack of transparency, many mills fake that they have ‘deadstock’ fabrics in order to appeal to their consumer market, companies and brands that promote sustainability in clothing. Likewise, many fast-fashion companies will continue to discard large amounts of fabric because there are other brands that are willing to repurpose that discarded fabric. There is a lack of accountability and responsibility coming from these larger companies that exploit the efforts of those who contribute to less waste in the fashion industry. Nonetheless, deadstock fabric can be a relatively sustainable option for businesses that want to delve into environmentally friendly alternatives to improve their brands' ecological footprint.
